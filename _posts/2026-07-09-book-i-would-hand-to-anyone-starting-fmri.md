I thought it would be logical to start with a post about what to do once you've
collected your pilot fMRI data, and how to check its quality. But then I
realized a text like this one would actually fit better for the very first post.

I'm doing fMRI research at the moment, and there's always something special in
it for me. I first saw it during one of the classes in my master's program, and
I remember really wanting it in my life. It may sound cheesy, but I was watching
*Grey's Anatomy* then, and I was dreaming about how I could do something so cool
that could help people. After that, time passed, I started doing it during my
PhD, got used to it, the first bloom of wonder faded, I collected data for a
year, and it became more of a routine. Analysis, I feel, though, stayed
something you can keep improving on, constantly, without ever ending. I enjoy
searching for resources where people comprehensively explain fMRI, and at some
point I realized I enjoy such blogs so much that I'd like to make my own, a
place to collect the things I keep using and coming back to. So this is how this
blog started to live. That's its whole idea: I want to share the great resources
people have made and genuinely shared, because I think more people should know
about great things. Another reason I'm doing this blog is that I want to publish 
the code I've worked on and figured out the parameter choices for myself. For most
of what I'll post, I went through each parameter one by one, so I thought 
it might be useful for someone to see another person's code while they're working
on their own. Sometimes, when I was trying to find solutions for my research, 
it was hard to come across example code to look at - and that's one of the main 
reasons I want to share mine.

Thinking back to three years ago when I was wondering what to read to start
understanding MRI/fMRI. After wandering through many resources, blogs, and
tutorials, I came across a book called *Handbook of Functional MRI Data
Analysis* by Poldrack, Mumford, and Nichols (Cambridge University Press, 2011).
Because the author list had Jeanette Mumford in it, and since I have a big
personal appreciation for this researcher and her channel about statistics and
fMRI (<https://www.youtube.com/@mumfordbrainstats>), seeing her name there
immediately gave me the feeling that I'd love this book. So I ordered it on
Amazon.

The most important thing for me about this book is how it's delivered. I've read
popular manuals on statistical inference for fMRI, and many of them were hard to
follow. They are correct, and they are still solid references I come back to
when I need them. But if you're at the point where you just want to grasp an
intuitive understanding of what is going on, this book gives you exactly that
because it reads almost like a story. It explains why each step exists, what
your options are, what you should consider, and what the state of the research
was at the time (it was written in 2011, but a lot of the basics have stayed the
same and still hold up). So as you read, you actually start to understand what's
happening and why you'd make one choice over another. I came across this book
after I had already covered the basics, so I can say now that it's full of the
things you'll actually run into once you start analyzing fMRI data. You'll have
to make choices about parameters, and a lot of the time those choices are
somewhat arbitrary, which is exactly why I believe the first thing to do is
understand the *why* before the *how*.

Another thing I like is that it gives you options when it comes to software.
There is no single "right" tool, so it walks through the differences between
packages like FSL, SPM, and AFNI using real examples, so you get a feel for what
each one is good at and which you might want to reach for. Finally, it is full of
references, so whenever a topic grabs you and you want to go deeper, you already
know where to look next.

It's the book I find most valuable for building up my intuitive understanding,
and I come back to it regularly. I bought a physical copy to keep as my go-to
desk book. It lives on my office table, sometimes goes home with me, and has
even come along on trips.
